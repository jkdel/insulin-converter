Equine Insulin Converter

Background

In equine medicine, measuring insulin and accurately interpreting the results is most important when dealing with the equine metabolic syndrome (EMS) or pituitary pars intermedia dysfunction (PPID), formerly known as equine cushing syndrome. The increased risk for laminitis associated with these diseases is mediated by insulin dysregulation (ID), which encompasses basal and post-prandial hyperinsulinemia as well as insulin resistance.

The assays available to measure equine insulin vary widely in their biochemical properties (type of assay, sensitivity and specifity, robustness against matrix effects or dilution, range of quantification, etc.), and could never be compared to a common gold standard using a large scale standardized approach for lack of recombinant equine insulin or sufficiently large sets of publicly available pooled samples. Still, very different assays were used to determine the reference ranges for horses or cut-offs for the wealth of published diagnostic tests.

To enable the comparison between samples and cut-offs determined using any combination of assays for which we had sufficient data, we built this publicly available WebApp. The methodology for determining the non-linear conversion functions is described in detail in a paper to be published soon. Briefly, a polynomial function of degree 1 to 10 was estimated using cross-validation and data from samples in which insulin was measured using two or more assays.

The combinations of assays for which the conversion is currently possible is presented in figure 1.

To use the App enter the insulin concentration in µIU/mL measured using any of the available assays and click "convert". The App will then provide the predicted insulin concentration for any other assay, as far as possible based on our data. These values are provided without any warrantie as mere approximations. Please note that decisions for treatment should always consider the specific clinical case and not be based solely on laboratory values - even less after a conversion carrying additional uncertainty.

More information

Insulin dysregulation is a central part of the Equine Metabolic Syndrome and can be detected using OGT. However, it might also occur in horses affected by Pars Pituitary Intermedia Deficiency (PPID), formerly known as Equine Cushing Syndrome. This is why horses that where found positive in the OGT and could possibly be affected by PPID (age, other clinical signs) should also be tested (e.g. by TRH stimulation test), and if necessary treated, for this disease.

The management of patients with EMS mostly consists in an optimization of the diet and exercise. Energy intake has to be reduced gradually and adapted to the horse's actual needs, which are sometimes difficult to evaluate for the owner. Exercising of the horse is not limited to a more intense training but also includes changes in the stables like access to a paddock, more space for social interaction or putting obstacles in the paddock to make the route from roughage to water circuitous etc. More specific advices and suggestions can be found in literature.
